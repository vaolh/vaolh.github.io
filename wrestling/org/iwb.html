International Wrestling Board

The International Wrestling Board (こくさいレスリングひょうぎかい,Kokusai Resuringu Hyōgikai) (IWB) is an international professional wrestling organization based in Japan. It is among the three major organizations which sanction professional wrestling bouts, alongside the World Wrestling Federation (WWF) and the World Wrestling Organization (WWO). The IWB sanctions championships across four men’s divisions: Welterweight, Middleweight, Bridgerweight, and Heavyweight; and two women’s divisions: Lightweight and Featherweight. Its prestigious belts trace their design lineage to the “Big Green Belt” version of the WWF World Heavyweight Championship (1983–1984), which was introduced during Hulk Hogan’s Japan tour and later used as the basis for the original Triple Crown title. When the IWB established its own championship system, the design was adapted into six new titles with red straps, now collectively referred to as the “Big Red Belt.” Governance is carried out by a General Assembly of officials, promoters, trainers, and wrestlers, which oversees rules, rankings, mandatory challengers, and championship recognition.

The IWB traces its origins to the post-war rise of Japanese professional wrestling. Pioneered by Rikidōzan in the 1950s and expanded by Giant Baba with the founding of All Japan Pro Wrestling (AJPW) in 1972, Japan developed multiple regional promotions and governing bodies, many of which operated as independent commissions rather than commercial promotions. The International Wrestling Grand Prix (IWGP, インターナショナル・レスリング・グラン・プリ) functioned as New Japan Pro-Wrestling’s governing body, managing rankings, championship authority, and match oversight. Following the collapse of the NWA territory system and the decline of other regional alliances, AJPW and NJPW merged administratively, and the IWGP was renamed the International Wrestling Board, consolidating governance over Japanese wrestling. The IWB standardized rules, championship recognition, and matchmaking, and over time became recognized internationally as the principal sanctioning authority for Japanese professional wrestling.

In 2020, the IWB, WWF, and IWB reached an agreement to standardize professional wrestling rules. The deal created a unified ruleset for matches, officiating, and discipline, while each body created new world championships across four men’s and two women’s divisions, with tag team and trios titles discontinued. The Ring magazine was recognized as an independent authority for lineal championships and pound-for-pound rankings. The agreement also established a shared calendar of events, including an annual openweight tournament (The Open), a trios tournament, and the bimonthly World Title Series, which features mandatory title defenses and contender matches from all three organizations.

The IWB favors strikers and strong-style competitors in its rankings and number-one contender matches, reflecting the organization’s emphasis on high-impact, hard-hitting wrestling. It organizes Japanese-held World Title Series, with events staged at historic venues such as Tokyo Dome, Nippon Budokan, Korakuen Hall, and Ryōgoku Kokugikan, showcasing the top talent from all three major sanctioning bodies. These events serve as the premier platform for championship defenses under the unified ruleset, while the IWB maintains centralized oversight of rankings, contenders, and title legitimacy.
