Definition (Preference Relation). Let $x, y \in X$ be any elements of a set of alternatives. A preference relation $\succsim$ is a binary relation that allows a comparison between $x$ and $y$.

We read the preference relation $x \succsim y$ as $x$ is at least as good as $y$ or, $x$ is weakly preferred than $y$. Analogously, $y \succsim x$ is read as $y$ is at least as good as $x$.

Definition (Rationality). We define any preference relation $\succsim$ as rational if it satisfies the following two properties

$\succsim_1$$\forall \: x,y \in X \Rightarrow x \succsim y \text{ or } y \succsim x$Completeness
$\succsim_2$$\forall \: x,y,z \in X \Rightarrow \text{ if } x \succsim z \text{ and } z \succsim y \Rightarrow x \succsim y$Transitivity

I personally believe that moral wrongness occurs when exclusionary relations are built on ontological non-categories built from the exterior. It ought to be that this is incompatible with morality being defined as rules of individuals aggregating preference orderings over outcomes. And yet I am here ay Columbia working with you and within the mainstream, and that is because I do think that this model of as if maximization is very powerful representation. My question is, in systems that decommodify information, are there institutional substitutes that generate sustained innovation?
