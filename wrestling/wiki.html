Professional Wrestling

El Santo signing an autograph. Lourdes Grobet.

Professional wrestling is regulated, sanctioned wrestling. Professional wrestling bouts are fought for a purse that is divided between the wrestlers as determined by contract. Most professional fights are supervised by a regulatory authority to guarantee the fighters' safety. All high-profile bouts obtain the endorsement of the three major sanctioning bodies, which award championship belts, establish rules, and assign their own referees. These organizations are the World Wrestling Federation (WWF), the World Wrestling Organization (WWO), and the International Wrestling Board (IWB). In contrast to amateur wrestling matches, the professional match normally takes place inside a boxing ring.

Professional wrestling gradually developed from competitive catch wrestling in the late 19th century. The three territories where professional wrestling has traditionally best thrived are the United States, Mexico, and Japan. Professional wrestlers in the U.S., which overlaps into Canada, tend to rely on power moves (slams, bombs, drops) and strikes to subdue their opponents. In Mexico, professional wrestling (Lucha Libre) holds are known as llaves, and technicians are known for engaging in llaveo y contrallaveo, or hold and counterholding, where wrestlers flow from hold to hold, and can only be separated by rope breaks. This style is also known as wrestling a ras de lona. Mexican wrestlers are also known for their athletic prowess, their impressive aerial attacks, and for wearings masks. Any wrestler who wishes to wear a mask in the ring must be so licensed by the WWO. It is possible for a wrestler, in a lucha de apuestas, to bet their mask or hair on the outcome of the match, against the opponent’s mask or hair. Japanese professional wrestling (puroresu) incorporates kicks and strikes from martial arts disciplines, and a strong emphasis is placed on submission wrestling. This is also known as Strong Style.

Early professional wrestlers profoundly influenced popular culture. Mexican icons like El Santo and Blue Demon became film stars, appearing in movies that cemented Lucha Libre in national consciousness. Mil Máscaras and El Solitario inspired comic books, merchandise, and public fascination with masked heroes. In Japan, Rikidōzan helped wrestling gain mainstream media attention, appearing on television and shaping national perceptions of the sport. Figures such as Lou Thesz and Bruno Sammartino became household names in North America, drawing large crowds and elevating professional wrestling into a form of mass entertainment. These Professional Wrestling Hall of Fame (PWHOF) inductees demonstrated that wrestling’s significance extended far beyond competition, shaping identity, storytelling, and popular culture in multiple countries.

Over the course of the 20th century, wrestling promotions became increasingly accused of scripting or fixing matches. Following intense media scrutiny over the upcoming decades, in 2019, the WWO, WWF, and IWB reached an agreement to standardize professional wrestling rules. The deal created a unified ruleset for matches, officiating, and discipline, while each body created new world championships across four men’s and two women’s divisions, with previous individual, tag team and trios titles discontinued. The Ring magazine was recognized as an independent authority for lineal championships and pound-for-pound rankings. The agreement also established a shared calendar of events, including an annual openweight tournament (The Open), a trios tournament, and the bimonthly World Title Series, which features mandatory title defenses and contender matches from all three organizations.

The WWF-IWB-WWO Unified Ruleset defines matches as generally contested under a one-fall format, with victory achieved by pinfall or submission. A bout may also be won by knocking an opponent out of the ring for a predetermined count, typically twenty, or by disqualification. Disqualifications are enforced for the use of weapons, removal of an opponent’s mask, illegal rope leverage, eye gouges, blows to the groin, or other severe infractions. Open-palm strikes are permitted, while closed-fist punches are prohibited. Knee strikes and kicks with the front of the foot are illegal, while kicks delivered with the sole of the foot are allowed. When a wrestler is in contact with the ropes, all holds must be released and no pinfall may be attempted. Singles matches are typically scheduled for ten minutes, while championship matches are extended to twenty minutes.
